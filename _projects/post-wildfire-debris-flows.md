As wildfire expands across the western United States, burned steeplands become primed for destructive debris flows during the first rains that follow. Our group has built a sustained program to understand and forecast these events. We have quantified how the rainfall thresholds that trigger post-fire debris flows vary with a region's underlying rainfall climatology, how fire perturbs the hydraulic properties of soil, and how to reliably distinguish dangerous runoff-generated debris flows from ordinary floods. {% cite east_watershed_2021 thomas_postfire_2023 thomas_rainfall_2023 delgado_quantification_2022 %}

These advances are increasingly informing operational hazard assessment: the rainfall thresholds and warning criteria used to protect communities downstream of burn scars. {% cite cavagnaro_robust_2024 cavagnaro_rainfall_2025 cavagnaro_improved_2025 %}

This work is carried out in close partnership with the USGS Landslide Hazards Program, the California Geological Survey, and NOAA, and is supported by awards from the USGS, NOAA, and NSF. A recent review with colleagues synthesizes the current state of post-fire debris-flow science. {% cite gartner_post-wildfire_2024 %}
